Description

Jellyfish are soft, translucent marine animals with a bell-shaped, gelatinous body and tentacles that trail behind them. They are part of the phylum Cnidaria and can be found in oceans worldwide, from the surface to the deep sea. Jellyfish move by contracting their bell, propelling themselves through the water. They are known for their ability to sting using specialized cells called nematocysts, which release toxins to capture prey or defend themselves. Despite their simple body structure, jellyfish have been around for over 500 million years, making them one of the oldest living creatures on Earth.

Behavior

Jellyfish exhibit a unique and mostly passive behavior in the ocean. They drift through the water, propelled by rhythmic contractions of their bell, allowing them to move with ocean currents. Jellyfish often gather in large groups, called blooms, which can be triggered by environmental factors like temperature and food availability. Despite their simplicity, jellyfish can display complex behaviors, such as swimming in sync with the tides or avoiding certain water conditions.
